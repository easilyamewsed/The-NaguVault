Okay — both are juicy, but they taste different, and the one you pick decides what the whole book feels like.

🗡️ Scenario 1 — “Malice at the Ritual”

  • Tone: tight, personal, almost murder-mystery. The “curse” isn’t cosmic law; it’s a crime scene.

  • Protagonist energy: Hibari’s “steal the stars” goal feels concrete because it’s one mage’s sabotage that ruined Kanato. Kanato’s tragedy is sharp and personal — somebody did this to him.

  • Plot shape: hunt the impostor ritualist, piece by piece; Kanato’s suffering is medical/political and can be undone. Akira’s role is clean: investigator. Seraph gets revenge fuel.

  • Payoff: when Hibari finally “steals the stars,” it’s more like cracking open a prison built by a single malicious hand. Cathartic and human.

Think: fantasy thriller + cursed prince + phantom thief romance.

🌌 Scenario 2 — “Cosmic Birdcage”

  • Tone: sweeping, mythic, fatalistic. The curse is the system itself; prosperity literally shackles the king.

  • Protagonist energy: Hibari’s heist feels wild and impossible — he’s fighting the architecture of the world. It’s rebellion vs destiny.

  • Plot shape: unravel ancestral sins and politics; the cost of keeping the kingdom alive; monsters spilling out if Kanato dies. Akira’s role is about rewriting ancient magic. Seraph becomes state-loyal knight forced to rethink everything.

  • Payoff: heavier and more tragic; “stealing the stars” is almost Prometheus-scale hubris. Great for cosmic horror undertones or generational guilt.

Think: epic dark fairytale + doomed heir + cosmic law-breaking thief romance.

💡 Quick gut check:

  • Want a tighter, character-driven mystery and personal revenge arc → Scenario 1.

  • Want big myth, destiny, world-altering rebellion, themes of prosperity vs personal freedom → Scenario 2.

If you love your “birdcage curse” metaphor, Scenario 2 embodies it perfectly (the throne is literally a cage). If you want Hibari’s theft to feel more heist vs human villain, Scenario 1’s your jam.
